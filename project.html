In extrapolating an online chess database of 721,983 games, I have visualized a total sum of 112,073 games that make up the top thirty most common endgames — the chart on the left shows the remaining pieces for each side when the game ends. Though since this data spans from 1475 to 2014, this graph may not be entirely pertinent for the modern metagame, but still holds value for the history of chess and the game itself. Also, since the database's average Elo ranking is 2199, any analysis here may not apply much for beginners or novices, or any games between players with a significant difference in Elo.

Now then, this visualization holds many interesting details just waiting to be dissected and analysed.

The Sustainability of Pieces

This graph shows a piece's sustainability — the likeliness a piece will survive until the endgame. The king always survive since that piece cannot be eaten, while none of the common endgames ended with a second copy of a knight, bishop, and rook. If you are winning, pawn are basically guaranteed to have at least one pawn in your arsenal. If you have a rook, your enemy is likely to have a rook; this applies vice versa, and such a mirror scenario occurs for the players' possession of their respective queens. Winning side has a little over 11% in sustainability for their bishops than the losing side's, while the losing side's is 8% more likely to live. This information speaks to the common theory that bishops work better than knights, on their own, as knights synergize with the movements and positions of the pawns, and as the game progresses, both sides start losing their pawns.

The Difference in Point Advantage

The data also reveals that points matter. In analysing the game of chess, past scholars have made a standardized value system that nominates points for all the pieces of the game. Pawns are worth 1 each, bishops and knights are worth are 3 each, rooks are worth 5 each, the queen is worth 9, and the king is worth 1. So each player starts with 39 in total, and each lose points as each lose each's respective pieces. The graph shows that the winning side usually either 1 or 2 points ahead, or both sides are tied.
